## Context

A traveler medical case is rarely complex clinically — it is complex operationally. The traveler is outside their home system, often in a hotel rather than a clinic, and the assistance desk is reading notes from across the Mediterranean. Language, expectations, and documentation standards all add friction before a single clinical decision is made.

## Key points

- **Language barrier at intake.** German-speaking reception is required to take a clean history, otherwise triage time stretches and notes arrive incomplete.
- **Fragmented billing on arrival.** Without a fixed-scope framework, charges land as multiple line items that require individual review by the assistance desk.
- **Unclear escalation paths.** When inpatient referral is needed, travelers and assistance teams need one named contact, not a switchboard.
- **Traveler stress shapes outcomes.** Anxiety about coverage drives unnecessary calls back to ADAC and slows clinical cooperation at the bedside.
- **Claim disputes on consumables.** Itemized invoices generate retroactive queries on dressings, observation time, and ancillary tests.

## What this means for ADAC

These are the exact friction points that determine whether a case closes in 48 hours or runs for weeks. Addressing them at the point of care — language, scope, escalation — shortens the claims cycle and reduces the active caseload carried by the assistance team.
